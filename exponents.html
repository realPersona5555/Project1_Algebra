Did You Know?

Although mathematicians have used exponents since at least the 9th century, the actual word "exponent" was not coined until 1544 by Michael Stifel, and our modern way of writing exponents was introduced by Rene Descartes in the 17th century.

Exponents

Exponents
– Tells you how many times you should multiply that number by itself.

32 can be written as 3 * 3
45 can be written as 4 * 4 * 4 * 4 * 4
Writing the same number being multiplied multiple times is tedious, we use exponents instead.

xy

x is the base.
y is the exponent.
Means to multiply x by itself y times.

  • 45 is read as 4 to the power of 5/5th power.
  • 22 is read as 2 squared/to the power of 2/2nd power.
  • 33 is read as 3 cubed/to the power of 3/3rd power.

Examples

  • 45 = 1,024
  • 22 = 4
  • 33 = 81

Negative Exponents

Sometimes you can have negative exponents. In this case what do you do?

Simple, instead of multiplying the number by itself x times, you divide 1 by that number x times.

2-3 can be understood as:
1 ÷ (2 × 2 × 2) = 0.125

Examples

  • 4-3 = 0.015625
  • 2-2 = 0.25
  • 3-3 = 0.037

Laws of Exponents

There's multiple rules to keep in mind when handling exponents. Here's a few common laws you need to remember.

x1 = x
x0 = 1
x-1 = 1 ÷ x
xmxn = xm + n
xm ÷ xn = xm - n

Examples

  • 45 × 45 = 410
  • 22 ÷ 22 = 20
  • 31 = 3

Recap

An exponent makes writing repeated multiplication easier by compressing all of it into one term. It tells us how many times to multiply a number(the base) by itself or divide 1 by that number if it is a negative exponent.
