Did You Know?

Algebra has been around since the days of the ancient Babylonians; however, algebraic expressions, as well as ways to evaluate, simplify, and balance them were first written down by Persian mathematician al-Khwarizmi in his highly influential book al-Jabr. This book is so-influential that European universities used it to teach students for over 500 years.

Expressions

Expression vs Equations

An expression and equation look the same at first glance. They both contain numbers, operations and/or variables. The best way to differentiate if it is an expression or an equation is if it contains an equal sign (=). If it has an equal sign, it is an equation, if it does not it is just an expression.

Expression – A phrase that contains numbers, letters with operators to represent a value.

Equation – A statement of equality. It contains 2 expressions connected by an equal sign.

2 + 3 is an expression.
2 + 3 = 9 – 4 is an equation.

Variable – A letter that represents a number wherein it’s value may change.

Constant – A number where it’s value never changes and always stays the same.

Inequality – An expression comparing two values with the usage of various inequality symbols (≠, <, ≤, >, ≥).

Grouping Symbols – Used to determine which expressions are to be kept together. More will be discussed during the lesson on PEMDAS and Order of Operations.

Numerical Expressions – Expressions that contain numbers and operators without any unknown variables or inequality symbols.

Algebraic Expressions – Expressions that contain numbers and operators as well as variables without inequality symbols.

Examples

  • 40 + 80 is a numerical expression.
  • 5x + 9 is an algebraic expression.
  • 30 + 5 = 45 – 10 is an equation.

Simplifying Algebraic Expressions

When you see a really long expression with numbers and letters in it, you would feel sightly intimidated, but no worries, most of those long expressions can be simplified into shorter ones!

To simplfy an algebraic expression, you simply need to combine like-terms.

What are like terms you may ask? Let's first discuss what a term is.

Term - Can be a single variable, a number of a variable and a number multipied together.

In the expression 4x + 5, 4x and 5 are both terms in the expression.

Now for like terms, those are terms with the same variable and/or exponents, they are alike with each other.

4x and 2x are like terms as they both have a common variable of x.
4x and 2x2 however are NOT like terms as they differ in exponents despite having the same variable.

What does it mean to combine like terms?

When you have 2 like terms separated by an addition or subtraction symbol, you can perform the operation on the numerical part of the terms and ignore the variables for now, and add them back afterwards. If the like terms are separated by a multiplication or division symbol, you may need to square(multipliction) or completely remove the variable (division).

  • 4x + 2x can be written into 6x
  • 4x - 2x can be written into 2x
  • 4x ÷ 2x can be written into 2
  • 4x × 2x can be written into 8x2

Examples

Let's try simplifying the following equation:
4x + 2x - (2y + 7y)

Let's identify what are the like terms present in the expression. We can see 4x and 2x as well as 2y and 7y.
Now let's combine those like terms together.

6x - 9y

And there we have it! The simpletest form of the expression: 4x + 2x - (2y + 7y).

Evaluting Algebraic Expressions

To evaluate an algebraic expression, you are to calculate for its exact value. To do that, you just have to substitute the variables with the values they stand for.

Examples

Evaluate 4x + 3y - 2z wherein x = 3 | y = 2 | z = 1

Let's first substitute all the variables for numbers.
4(3) + 3(2) - 2(1)

Now we go about it calculating like a normal expression.
12 + 6 - 2
18 - 2
16

Recap

This lesson covers a lot, so let's break it down into 3 parts; expressions vs equations, simplifying expressions, and evaluating expressions.

Firstly, the easiest way to tell apart an expression from an equation is that equations must have an equals (=) sign. 4x + 5 is just an expression, but if we turn it into 4x + 5 = 3, then it is now an equation.

That's right! Onto the second point, simplifying an expression is just a matter of combining like terms. For example, to simplify 4x + 5 - 8 - 2x, we would first combine 4x and -2x by adding them up to form 2x. Then, we would add 5 and -8 to form -3. Thus, the simplified expression is 2x - 3.

Lastly, evaluating an expression involves substituting a variable such as x with a given value.
For example, if we were to evaluate 2x - 3 given that x = 4, then we would simply replace x with 4 to get 2(4) - 3 = 8 - 3 = 5.
